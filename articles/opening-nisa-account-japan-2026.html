Opening Your NISA Account in Japan (2026): A Practical Guide

Updated April 16, 2026

Getting Started with NISA in Japan (2026)

For foreigners living in Japan, the NISA (Nippon Individual Savings Account) system offers a powerful way to invest tax-free. As of 2026, the updated NISA program provides much higher tax-exempt investment limits and a permanent tax-free period, making it an essential tool for long-term financial planning. This guide walks you through the practical steps of choosing a brokerage and opening your NISA account.

Step 1: Confirming Your NISA Eligibility

Before diving into brokerage options, ensure you meet the fundamental requirements for opening a NISA account:

  • Residency Status: You must be a tax resident (税法上の居住者 - zeihōjō no kyojūsha) in Japan. This generally means you have a valid residence card and live in Japan.
  • Age: You must be 18 years or older as of January 1st of the year you open the account.
  • My Number: You need a My Number (個人番号 - kojin bangō), which is your individual identification number in Japan. This is important for tax reporting and account verification.

Common Mistake: Not having your My Number ready. Many foreigners delay getting their My Number Card (マイナンバーカード - My Number Card) or Notification Card (通知カード - Tsūchi Kādo). Brokerages cannot process your NISA application without it. If you only have the paper Notification Card, you will likely need another form of ID (like your Residence Card) to complete verification.

Step 2: Choosing Your NISA Brokerage

The choice of brokerage is critical, especially for beginners. While many financial institutions offer NISA accounts, online brokerages are typically the most convenient and cost-effective. For foreigners, two major players stand out due to their extensive product offerings and relatively streamlined online processes:

  • Rakuten Securities (楽天証券 - Rakuten Shōken): Often cited as user-friendly, Rakuten Securities integrates well with other Rakuten services (like Rakuten Bank and Rakuten Card). It offers a wide selection of low-cost index funds suitable for NISA. The platform is primarily in Japanese, but many users report finding online guides and community support helpful for navigation.
  • SBI Securities (SBI証券 - SBI Shōken): As Japan's largest online brokerage, SBI Securities offers an even broader range of investment products. It is highly competitive on fees and fund selection, including popular eMAXIS Slim series funds. While its interface can be slightly less intuitive for absolute beginners compared to Rakuten, it is a strong option for those comfortable with a bit more complexity.

Both Rakuten and SBI Securities offer excellent choices for NISA, providing access to the same popular, low-cost index funds. There are generally no account maintenance fees for NISA accounts at these online brokers. Other options like Monex Securities (マネックス証券 - Manekkusu Shōken) also exist but are less frequently discussed among the expat community for NISA purposes.

Step 3: Gathering Required Documents

Before you start the online application, collect the following:

  • Residence Card (在留カード - Zairyū Kādo): Your primary form of identification and proof of residency.
  • My Number Document: Either your My Number Card or your My Number Notification Card (along with another ID like your Residence Card).
  • Japanese Bank Account: You will need a Japanese bank account to link for deposits and withdrawals. This account must be in your name.
  • Hanko (印鑑 - inkan - personal seal): While many online brokerages now allow signature-based applications, some may still request a hanko for certain procedures or for traditional accounts. It is generally advisable to have one registered with your bank, though it may not be strictly necessary for the initial online brokerage application.

Step 4: The Online Application Process

Opening a NISA account is typically a two-step process:

  1. Open a Regular Brokerage Account: First, you must open a standard (taxable) brokerage account with your chosen institution. This involves filling out online forms, providing personal details, and uploading your identification documents (Residence Card, My Number document). The brokerage will verify your identity, often through an online portal or by sending documents via postal mail for signature.
  2. Apply for the NISA Account: Once your regular brokerage account is established, you will then apply for the NISA account *within* that brokerage's platform. This is a separate application. You will declare that you wish to open a NISA account and confirm your eligibility. The brokerage will then submit your NISA application to the National Tax Agency (国税庁 - Kokuzeichō) for approval.

What they don't tell you: The NISA approval takes time. The application for the NISA account itself is not instantly approved by the brokerage. It requires verification by the National Tax Agency to ensure you do not hold another NISA account with a different institution (you can only have one NISA account at a time). This process can take several weeks, typically ranging from two to four weeks, depending on the time of year and agency workload. You will receive notification from your brokerage once your NISA account is officially open and ready for investment.

Common Mistake: Forgetting the NISA application. Many beginners successfully open a regular brokerage account but overlook the important second step of applying for the NISA account. Without this, your investments will be in a taxable account, missing out on the tax-free benefits.

Step 5: Funding Your NISA Account

Once your NISA account is open, you will need to fund it. This is typically done by linking your Japanese bank account to your brokerage account. You can then transfer funds directly from your bank to your brokerage. For those transferring money from overseas, services like Wise or Revolut can be used to send funds to your Japanese bank account, which then feeds into your brokerage.

Step 6: Choosing Your NISA Investments

With funds in your account, the next step is to select your investments. For beginners, low-cost, broadly diversified index funds are highly recommended. These funds aim to track a market index (like the global stock market or the S&P 500) and require minimal active management, leading to lower fees.

Popular choices available through Rakuten and SBI Securities include:

  • eMAXIS Slim All-Country (オールカントリー - Ōru Kantorī): This fund invests in companies across developed and emerging markets worldwide, offering broad diversification. Its management fees are typically very low, often below 0.1% annually as of 2026.
  • eMAXIS Slim S&P 500: This fund tracks the performance of the S&P 500 index, investing in 500 of the largest U.S. companies. It also boasts very low management fees.

The NISA system in 2026 has two investment frames: the Growth Investment Frame (成長投資枠 - Seichō Tōshi Waku) and the Tsumitate Investment Frame (つみたて投資枠 - Tsumitate Tōshi Waku). Both can be used simultaneously. Low-cost index funds are eligible for both frames, allowing you to maximize your tax-free contributions.

Pro Tip: Set Up Automatic Recurring Investments Immediately

One of the most effective strategies for NISA is to set up automatic monthly investments (積立設定 - tsumitate settei) as soon as your account is active. This practice, known as dollar-cost averaging, involves investing a fixed amount regularly, regardless of market fluctuations. It removes the emotion from investing, prevents you from trying to time the market, and ensures you consistently contribute to your tax-free allowance. Both Rakuten and SBI Securities offer strong tools for setting up these recurring investments.

Conclusion

Opening a NISA account in Japan as a foreigner in 2026 is a straightforward process once you understand the steps and requirements. By choosing a reputable online brokerage like Rakuten Securities or SBI Securities, preparing your documents, and understanding the application timeline, you can effectively begin your journey toward tax-free investing. Start early, invest consistently in diversified, low-cost index funds, and use the power of the NISA system for your financial future in Japan.
