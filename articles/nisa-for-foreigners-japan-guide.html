NISA for foreigners in Japan: A foreigner's step-by-step guide

Updated April 14, 2026

NISA feels like a locked drawer

You’ve heard about NISA. Maybe from a coworker who casually mentions 10 percent returns. Or from a Reddit thread where someone brags about their 20 million yen portfolio. But when you try to open an account, the bank tells you you’re not eligible. Or the paperwork is in Japanese you can’t read. Or the minimum deposit is more than you can swing on a teacher’s salary. It feels like the system is designed to keep you out.

I’ve been there. Three years ago, I walked into a major branch of a Japanese bank with a pocket notebook full of questions. The staff smiled politely but kept handing me forms written in kanji I’d never seen. I left empty-handed and opened an overseas brokerage instead. Only later did I realize I’d missed the most powerful investment tool Japan offers to foreigners.

This guide is what I wish I’d had back then. Not a glossy brochure or a sales pitch. The actual steps, the real costs, and the mistakes that trip up almost everyone who tries this for the first time.

Step 1: Choose the right NISA flavor

Japan changed NISA rules in January 2024. The old “tsumitate NISA” is gone. Replaced by “NISA” and “tsumitate NISA” with new names: General NISA and Tsumitate NISA. The General NISA is for people who want to invest lump sums. Tsumitate NISA is for regular monthly contributions, which most foreigners actually need.

If you plan to deposit money every month, open the Tsuminate NISA account. If you have a large chunk of cash sitting in a Japanese savings account, the General NISA might work. But the monthly version is simpler for beginners and has lower paperwork.

You can only open one of each type. Once you choose, you can’t switch later. So pick the monthly one unless you’re certain you’ll stay in Japan for decades and have cash you won’t need for emergencies.

Step 2: Find a broker that accepts foreigners

The big three Japanese brokers—SMBc, Nomura, and Rakuten Securities—all claim to accept foreigners. In reality, Nomura and Rakuten often reject anyone without a Japanese phone number or permanent residency. SMBc is the safest bet. I opened my account there in 2023 after being rejected by two others.

If you already have a Japanese bank account and residence card, bring both to the branch. The staff will hand you a form titled “外国人投資家向け NISA 口座開設申込書.” It’s six pages long. Bring a pen. The Japanese sections are mandatory. The English sections are polite nods and legally meaningless.

If you’re on a spouse visa or student visa, the process is harder. Some branches will tell you outright that NISA is only for permanent residents. If that happens, walk to the next branch. Or contact SMBc’s foreigner-friendly desk (check their website for contact information) and ask for the “外国人投資家サポート窓口.” They know the trick of opening accounts for visa holders who can show proof of tax residency.

Step 3: Fill the forms without crying

The form asks for your name, address, tax number, and whether you’re a “high-net-worth individual.” Ignore the last question; it’s for millionaires only. The tricky part is the “投資経験” section. If you’ve ever bought a stock or ETF overseas, tick “あり.” If you’re brand new, tick “なし.” Either way, they’ll treat you the same.

They’ll also ask for a “マイナンバーカード” or your tax certificate. Bring both if you have them. If you don’t have a my number card, your residence card and passport usually suffice. The staff will photocopy everything. Expect the process to take 30 minutes to an hour.

One common mistake is signing in the wrong boxes. The form has two signature lines: one for opening the account, one for agreeing to the NISA rules. Newcomers often sign both at once. That triggers a rejection. Read the small print. Sign only where the staff points.

Step 4: Transfer money from overseas

Japan doesn’t let you wire money directly into a brokerage account. You need a Japanese bank account first. Wise and Revolut work, but only if you already have Japanese bank details. For most foreigners, the path is:

Overseas account → Wise/Rabobank → Japanese bank account → Brokerage account.

Wise charges about 0.4 percent per transfer. Revolut charges 0.5 percent for amounts under 10,000 USD. Both let you hold JPY and convert at the mid-market rate. Avoid banks that charge flat fees above 2,000 yen; the hidden markup on the exchange rate costs more.

A common mistake is waiting for the perfect exchange rate. The yen moves 2-3 percent in a single day. If you’re converting 5,000 USD, that’s 10,000 yen gone in one swing. Set a limit order at the rate you’re willing to accept and forget it. The money will arrive in 1-2 business days.

Step 5: Buy an index fund that actually exists

Once your brokerage account is funded, you need to choose an investment. The safest beginner choice is an S&P 500 index fund. In Japan, the one that fits is “iShares S&P 500 UCITS ETF.” Ticker: IUSA. It’s listed on the Tokyo Stock Exchange under the code 1553. Each share costs about 11,000 yen as of 2026. You can buy fractions, so even 5,000 yen gets you a slice.

If you want yen-hedged exposure, look for “iShares Core S&P 500 UCITS ETF (JP Yen-Hedged).” Ticker: I5SP. It’s more expensive—about 15,000 yen per share—but it protects you if the yen strengthens suddenly. Most foreigners ignore the hedging and accept the currency risk. That’s fine until the yen jumps 10 percent in a week.

A mistake I made was buying a Japan-only index fund. I thought it would be safer. Instead, it dropped 20 percent in 2024 while the S&P 500 rose. Diversification matters, even for beginners.

Step 6: Set up automatic monthly contributions

In the Tsumitate NISA account, you can set an automatic transfer from your linked bank account. The minimum is usually 1,000 yen per month. The broker will debit it on the date you choose and buy whatever fund you’ve set. No thinking required.

I set mine to 30,000 yen on the 25th of every month. The money leaves my account automatically. If the market is down, I get more shares. If it’s up, fewer. Over time, the average cost evens out. That’s dollar-cost averaging, and it’s the closest thing to a free lunch in investing.

A common mistake is timing the market. Beginners see a dip and cancel the automatic transfer to “wait for a better price.” The market usually rebounds before they log back in. Missing just two months of contributions can erase years of compounding.

Pro tip: Use the broker’s English app

SMBc’s app, “SMBC日興証券,” has a hidden English mode. Go to settings, scroll to “言語設定,” and toggle English on. The app becomes usable. The desktop site remains Japanese, but the app is your lifeline for checking balances and adjusting contributions on the go.

Most brokers don’t advertise this feature. Staff won’t mention it unless you ask. Once you find it, bookmark the login page. The English interface is clunky but functional. You’ll use it more than the Japanese version.

Taxes? Not your problem (yet)

NISA accounts are tax-free. No capital gains tax, no dividend tax, no reporting required. The broker handles everything. Even if you leave Japan, the account stays tax-free as long as you don’t sell anything. That’s the magic of NISA—until the government changes the rules again in 2037.

If you withdraw money before the five-year mark, you lose the tax benefit for that year. But the penalty is only on the withdrawn amount, not the whole portfolio. So if you need cash for an emergency, you can pull it out without destroying your plan.

What happens if you mess up

If you accidentally buy a non-NISA fund, the broker will flag it. You’ll have to sell it and buy a compliant fund within 30 days. If you miss the deadline, the fund becomes taxable outside NISA, which defeats the purpose. The broker sends email reminders, but they’re easy to ignore.

If you close the account too soon, you lose the tax-free status for that year. The clock resets when you reopen, so it’s best to keep the account open even if you stop contributing.

One last thing

The NISA system feels opaque because it was designed for Japanese investors. The forms, the fees, the fund selection—all assume you speak the language and live here permanently. But once you crack the first layer, the rest is routine. The hardest part is walking into that first branch and not walking out empty-handed.

I still remember the day my Tsumitate NISA account finally showed a positive balance. It was only 10,000 yen. But the system had worked. The money moved. The fund bought shares. No one asked for my visa status. No one cared if my Japanese was fluent. The drawer had opened after all.

That’s the real power of NISA—not the returns, not the tax savings, but the quiet proof that the system can bend for foreigners who try.
